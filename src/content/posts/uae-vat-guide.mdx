The UAE introduced Value Added Tax (VAT) on 1 January 2018 at a standard rate of 5%. Even at that low rate, the compliance obligations are real: registration thresholds, quarterly returns, input tax rules, and penalties for errors. As someone who prepares and files UAE VAT returns for multiple business clients every quarter, I've seen the same mistakes made repeatedly. This guide covers the essentials.

## Who Needs to Register for VAT?

VAT registration is managed by the Federal Tax Authority (FTA) through its EmaraTax portal. There are two registration types:

| Registration Type | Taxable Turnover Threshold | Obligation |
|---|---|---|
| Mandatory | AED 375,000 or more | Must register within 30 days |
| Voluntary | AED 187,500 – AED 374,999 | Can choose to register |
| Not eligible | Below AED 187,500 | Cannot register |

The threshold is calculated on taxable supplies (standard-rated + zero-rated) made or expected over a rolling 12-month period.

> **Practical tip:** Even below the mandatory threshold, voluntary registration is worth considering if you have significant input VAT on purchases. Once registered, you can reclaim that tax.

## Understanding VAT Categories

### Standard-Rated — 5%

The default rate for most goods and services. If a supply isn't specifically zero-rated or exempt, it's standard-rated.

### Zero-Rated — 0%

VAT is charged at 0%, but the supplier *can still reclaim input tax* on related costs. Key zero-rated supplies include:

- Exports of goods outside the UAE
- International transport of passengers and goods
- Certain healthcare and qualifying educational services
- Newly constructed residential property (first supply only)
- Investment-grade precious metals

### Exempt

No VAT is charged, and input tax on costs related to exempt supplies **cannot be reclaimed**. Exempt supplies include:

- Margin-based financial services
- Life insurance and reinsurance
- Bare land
- Local passenger transport

The distinction between zero-rated and exempt is critical and commonly confused — zero-rated lets you recover input tax, exempt does not.

## Filing VAT Returns

UAE VAT returns are typically quarterly, with the deadline falling **28 days after the end of the tax period**. Filing is done via EmaraTax using form VAT 201.

The return captures:
- **Output tax** — VAT charged on taxable supplies
- **Input tax** — VAT paid on purchases and expenses (recoverable)
- **Adjustments** — credit notes, bad debt relief, corrections
- **Net payable** — the difference between output and recoverable input tax

If input tax exceeds output tax, you're in a refund position. Most businesses carry it forward rather than requesting a refund, to avoid the administrative overhead.

## UAE Corporate Tax

Since June 2023, the UAE also applies Corporate Tax:

- **0%** on taxable income up to AED 375,000
- **9%** on taxable income above AED 375,000
- Qualifying Free Zone entities may access a 0% rate on qualifying income (subject to conditions)

CT registration is separate from VAT. The return is due within 9 months of financial year-end.

## 5 Common VAT Mistakes

1. **Claiming input tax on non-business expenses** — personal costs and exempt-related costs are not recoverable.
2. **Missing the 28-day filing deadline** — the first penalty is AED 1,000, rising to AED 2,000 for repeat offences, plus interest on late payments.
3. **Late registration** — the FTA will back-assess VAT from the date you should have registered, at your cost.
4. **Confusing zero-rated and exempt** — always check whether input tax recovery applies before treating a supply as "no VAT."
5. **Incorrect valuation of related-party supplies** — supplies between connected parties must be at fair market value for VAT purposes.

## Getting It Right

Clean bookkeeping, proper supply classification, and timely filing are the foundation. Cloud software (Xero, QuickBooks, Zoho Books) all have UAE VAT reporting built in — once your tax codes are set correctly, generating the data for EmaraTax takes minutes, not hours.
